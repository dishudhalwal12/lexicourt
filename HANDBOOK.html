Section 1

Why This Needed to Exist

  • Pre-hearing panic hits when I spend an hour flipping through a thick file just to remember what the last order said.
  • Paper sprawl means one missing page, one loose notice, or one buried date can throw off my entire hearing prep.
  • Draft fatigue wastes time because affidavits, notices, and applications keep getting rebuilt from scratch.
  • Client visibility breaks down when updates live inside my notebook, my memory, and a pile of documents no one else can read quickly.

Section 2

The Product

LexiCourt is a browser-based legal workspace where a lawyer can create a matter, upload records, ask an AI assistant direct questions about those files, pull a short summary, extract hearing dates, generate a first draft, and check whether the folder still has gaps before court.

This feels different from a generic college build because the product was shaped around hearing-readiness pressure, not around a fake dashboard with legal labels on top.

  • Matter Workspace - creates case folders and keeps each matter organized around one clear record.
  • Document Vault - stores orders, affidavits, notices, and supporting files inside the right case context.
  • AI Case Chat - answers plain-language questions from uploaded legal documents.
  • Summary Snapshots - turns long judgments and orders into quick reading before a hearing.
  • Timeline View - pulls dates, orders, and milestones into one readable sequence.
  • Draft + Readiness Desk - creates first-pass legal drafts and flags what may still be missing.

Section 3

The Users

Role-based access keeps the product focused: the lawyer does the heavy work, the client gets clarity, and the admin keeps a small practice under control.

Lawyer

  • Creates and manages case folders for each active matter.
  • Uploads documents, asks AI questions, and pulls summaries or timelines.
  • Generates drafts and checks missing-document alerts before court.

Main screen: the Lawyer Dashboard with case cards, recent matters, and quick actions.

Client

  • Checks shared updates without touching legal editing tools.
  • Views hearing dates, summaries, and the documents the lawyer chooses to share.
  • Gets a cleaner sense of progress without calling for every small update.

Main screen: a Client Dashboard with case status, next dates, and shared files.

Admin

  • Manages user accounts and access permissions inside a small practice.
  • Tracks flagged matters, storage usage, and team activity.
  • Reviews missing-document alerts across the practice when needed.

Main screen: an Admin Dashboard with user control, flagged folders, and top-level counts.

Section 5

The Flow

  1. Sign in and land on a dashboard that shows your matters, recent work, and the next place to click.
  2. Create a case folder, add matter details, and turn a loose pile of papers into one structured record.
  3. Upload orders, affidavits, notices, and supporting files, then watch the folder fill out like a working brief.
  4. Ask the AI what happened last, what the order says, or which dates matter, and get the answer inside the same screen.
  5. Generate a summary, a timeline, or a first draft so the lawyer sees usable text instead of raw document bulk.
  6. Check readiness before court and spot what still feels thin while there is still time to fix it.

Section 7

What's Not Done Yet

  • OCR is missing, so weak scans and handwritten pages still break text extraction.
  • AI output needs review, because drafts and summaries are first-pass work, not filing-ready legal text.
  • Court-system links are absent, so LexiCourt stops at internal preparation instead of direct e-Courts or ICMS submission.

Section 8

What's Next

  • Build OCR support so scanned orders and handwritten notes can enter the same document pipeline.
  • Add Hindi and Punjabi support so the product fits the language reality of North Indian legal work.
  • Connect shared-team workflows and later e-filing paths so a matter can move from preparation to action with less copying.

Section 9

Why This Mattered

LexiCourt turned courtroom admin into one working workspace: folders, uploads, AI answers, summaries, timelines, drafts, and readiness checks under the same roof. Building it taught us that product work starts with a sharp problem, then survives on prompt tuning, database discipline, and stubborn testing. We also learned that software feels different when it is shaped around somebody's actual bad day. If this saves a lawyer even one hour before a hearing, it has already earned its place.
